Week 1 - Probability Foundations

Sample spaces and events

A sample space S contains all possible outcomes. An event is a subset of S. Use unions for OR, intersections for AND, complements for NOT, and set differences for A but not B. De Morgan's laws: (A∪B)^c=A^c∩B^c and (A∩B)^c=A^c∪B^c.

Common misconceptions

Do not confuse an outcome with an event; do not assume OR is exclusive; define whether order matters.

Understanding check

Explain why the complement is useful for 'at least one' questions.

Counting and probability rules

Addition principle applies to mutually exclusive cases. Multiplication principle applies across stages. Ordered selections use permutations n!/(n-r)!; unordered selections use combinations C(n,r)=n!/[r!(n-r)!]. Probability obeys P(S)=1, P(A)≥0, P(A∪B)=P(A)+P(B)-P(A∩B), and P(A^c)=1-P(A).

Common misconceptions

Do not add overlapping probabilities without subtracting the intersection. Do not use combinations when order matters.

Understanding check

When is P(A∪B)=P(A)+P(B) valid?

Week 2 - Conditional Probability and Bayes

Conditional probability

For P(A)>0, P(B|A)=P(A∩B)/P(A). Conditioning restricts the sample space to outcomes where A occurred. Multiplication rule: P(A∩B)=P(A)P(B|A).

Common misconceptions

P(A|B) and P(B|A) are different. Never assume conditioning leaves probability unchanged unless independence is established.

Understanding check

Give an example where P(A|B) is very different from P(B|A).

Independence

A and B are independent when P(A∩B)=P(A)P(B). Equivalently, P(A|B)=P(A) when probabilities are positive. Mutually exclusive non-impossible events are not independent.

Common misconceptions

Mutually exclusive is not the same as independent. Pairwise independence need not imply mutual independence.

Understanding check

Can two positive-probability mutually exclusive events be independent?

Total probability and Bayes

For a partition B1,...,Bk, P(A)=ΣP(Bi)P(A|Bi). Bayes: P(Bj|A)=P(Bj)P(A|Bj)/ΣP(Bi)P(A|Bi). Priors are updated by likelihoods to form posteriors.

Common misconceptions

Do not ignore base rates in testing problems. Test sensitivity is not the same as probability of disease given a positive result.

Understanding check

Why can a highly accurate test produce many false positives for a rare condition?

Week 3 - Random Variables

Random variables and distributions

A random variable maps outcomes to real numbers. Discrete variables have finite or countable ranges; continuous variables vary over intervals. For a discrete X, pX(x)=P(X=x) and ΣpX(x)=1. For continuous X, probabilities are areas under a PDF and P(X=x)=0.

Common misconceptions

Do not treat a continuous density value as a point probability. Distinguish X, the random variable, from x, a realised value.

Understanding check

Why may a PDF exceed 1 even though probabilities cannot?

CDF, expectation, and variance

FX(x)=P(X≤x). For continuous X, FX(x)=∫[-∞,x]fX(t)dt. E[X]=ΣxpX(x) or ∫xfX(x)dx. Var(X)=E[X²]-(E[X])². E[aX+b]=aE[X]+b; Var(aX+b)=a²Var(X).

Common misconceptions

E[g(X)] is generally not g(E[X]). Variance scales by a², not a. Standard deviation is the square root of variance.

Understanding check

Why does adding a constant change the mean but not the variance?

Week 4 - Joint Distributions and Dependence

Joint, marginal, and conditional distributions

A random vector (X,Y) records two numerical features. Marginals are obtained by summing or integrating the joint distribution over the other variable. Conditional distributions divide the joint distribution by the relevant marginal on valid support.

Common misconceptions

Marginals do not uniquely determine the joint distribution. Always respect the support region.

Understanding check

How do you obtain fX(x) from fX,Y(x,y)?

Independence, covariance, correlation

X and Y are independent if the joint distribution factors into the product of marginals. Cov(X,Y)=E[XY]-E[X]E[Y]. Var(X+Y)=Var(X)+Var(Y)+2Cov(X,Y). Correlation standardises covariance and lies from -1 to 1. Independence implies zero covariance, but not conversely in general.

Common misconceptions

Zero correlation does not prove independence. Correlation measures linear association, not causation or every nonlinear pattern.

Understanding check

Construct a dependent pair with zero covariance.

Week 5 - Discrete Models

Binomial

X~Bin(n,p) counts successes in n independent Bernoulli trials with constant p. P(X=x)=C(n,x)p^x(1-p)^(n-x), E[X]=np, Var(X)=np(1-p).

Common misconceptions

Check fixed n, two outcomes, constant p, and independence. Sampling without replacement from a small population is usually not binomial.

Understanding check

List the four binomial assumptions.

Geometric and negative binomial

Geometric counts trials until the first success; negative binomial counts trials until the kth success. Under the trial-count convention, E[Geom(p)]=1/p and E[NB(k,p)]=k/p. Geometric is memoryless.

Common misconceptions

Be clear whether the convention counts failures or trials. Not every waiting-time distribution is memoryless.

Understanding check

Why does memorylessness matter in repeated-trial problems?

Poisson

X~Poi(λ) counts independent events in a fixed interval at constant average rate. P(X=x)=e^-λ λ^x/x!, E[X]=Var(X)=λ. Rates scale with interval length. Independent Poisson counts add, and thinning changes λ to pλ.

Common misconceptions

Do not forget to rescale λ for a different interval. A matching mean and variance alone does not prove a Poisson process.

Understanding check

When is a Poisson approximation to Bin(n,p) reasonable?

Week 6 - Continuous Models

Uniform

X~U(a,b) has density 1/(b-a) on [a,b], mean (a+b)/2, and variance (b-a)²/12. Probability is interval length divided by total length.

Common misconceptions

Each individual point still has probability zero. Do not forget the total interval length.

Understanding check

Why is P(X=x)=0 although X must take some value?

Exponential

X~Exp(λ) has density λe^-λx for x≥0, mean 1/λ, variance 1/λ², and memorylessness P(X>s+t|X>s)=P(X>t). It models waiting times in a Poisson process.

Common misconceptions

λ is a rate, not the mean. Memorylessness is a strong modelling assumption.

Understanding check

Convert a mean waiting time into λ.

Normal and approximation

X~N(μ,σ²). Standardise using Z=(X-μ)/σ. Normal approximation to binomial is suitable when np and n(1-p) are sufficiently large, with continuity correction.

Common misconceptions

Do not confuse σ with σ². Apply continuity correction when approximating discrete probabilities.

Understanding check

Explain the continuity correction for P(X≤k).

Week 8 - Sampling, LLN, and CLT

Population, sample, statistic

A population is the full collection of interest; a sample is a subset. A simple random sample is selected by a random mechanism. A statistic is a function of sample data and is random before observing the sample; a parameter describes the population.

Common misconceptions

A parameter is not a statistic. Convenience samples are not automatically representative.

Understanding check

Distinguish μ from X̄.

Sampling distribution and standard error

For iid observations with mean μ and variance σ²: E[X̄]=μ, Var(X̄)=σ²/n, and SE(X̄)=σ/√n. Standard error is the repeated-sampling spread of an estimator, not the spread of individual data.

Common misconceptions

Do not confuse standard deviation with standard error. A larger sample does not change population variability.

Understanding check

What happens to SE when n is multiplied by 4?

LLN and CLT

The LLN says X̄ approaches μ as n increases. The CLT says (X̄-μ)/(σ/√n) is approximately standard normal for sufficiently large n under suitable conditions. It is the sampling distribution of the mean that becomes approximately normal.

Common misconceptions

The raw data do not become normal. CLT is not exact for every small sample or dependent dataset.

Understanding check

Contrast the LLN and CLT.

Week 9 - Sampling Distributions and Estimation

Chi-square

For a normal sample, (n-1)S²/σ²~χ²(n-1). Chi-square distributions are nonnegative and right-skewed.

Common misconceptions

Use n-1 degrees of freedom and keep S² and σ² squared. The exact result assumes normality.

Understanding check

What parameter can be inferred using this result?

Student t

For a normal sample with unknown σ, T=(X̄-μ)/(S/√n)~t(n-1). t is symmetric with heavier tails than normal and approaches normal as df grows.

Common misconceptions

The reason for t is estimated σ, not merely 'small sample'. Use n-1 df.

Understanding check

Why are t tails heavier?

F distribution

Ratios of independent scaled chi-square variables follow F distributions. Ratios of sample variances from normal populations lead to F methods. F is nonnegative and right-skewed.

Common misconceptions

Numerator and denominator degrees of freedom are ordered and cannot be swapped casually.

Understanding check

What does an unusually large variance ratio suggest?

Point estimation and sample size

An estimator is unbiased if its expectation equals the target parameter. For known σ, required n for margin E is n≥(zα/2 σ/E)², rounded up. More confidence or more precision needs more data.

Common misconceptions

Unbiased does not automatically mean low variance or best. Never round required n down.

Understanding check

Why must sample size be rounded up?

Week 10 - Confidence Intervals

One population mean

Known σ: X̄±zα/2 σ/√n. Unknown σ for a normal population: X̄±tα/2,n-1 S/√n. Confidence refers to the long-run success rate of the interval procedure, not a probability assigned to a fixed μ after calculation.

Common misconceptions

Do not say there is a 95% probability that μ lies in an already observed interval. Choose z or t from assumptions.

Understanding check

Give a correct interpretation of 95% confidence.

Two independent means

Estimate μ1-μ2 using X̄-Ȳ. With known unequal variances, SE=√(σ1²/n1+σ2²/n2). For large samples with unknown variances, replace σ values by sample estimates. Interpret the sign according to group order.

Common misconceptions

Do not reverse the subtraction order accidentally. Do not pool without an equal-variance assumption.

Understanding check

What does an interval entirely above zero imply?

Paired data

Paired designs use repeated measures or matched units. Form Di=Xi-Yi and perform one-sample inference on μD. Pairing can reduce variation caused by unit-to-unit differences.

Common misconceptions

Before/after data are not independent samples. Do not ignore natural pairs.

Understanding check

Why can pairing improve precision?

Week 11 - Hypothesis Testing

Hypotheses and test direction

A test compares H0 with H1. H0 normally includes equality and concerns a population parameter. H1 may be one-sided or two-sided and should be selected before viewing the result.

Common misconceptions

Do not choose direction after seeing data. Hypotheses should not be written about X̄.

Understanding check

Write hypotheses for testing whether μ>50.

Type I, Type II, and power

Type I error rejects a true H0 and has probability α. Type II error fails to reject a false H0 and has probability β for a specified alternative. Power is 1-β. Larger samples usually increase power.

Common misconceptions

Failure to reject is not proof of H0. Power depends on effect size and sample size.

Understanding check

Explain the trade-off between α and β.

Test statistics and decisions

For known σ, Z=(X̄-μ0)/(σ/√n). Reject when the statistic lies in the rejection region determined by α and H1. State conclusions as evidence about the population claim, not certainty.

Common misconceptions

Do not compare raw means with z critical values. Say 'fail to reject', not 'accept', H0.

Understanding check

What determines whether one or two tails are used?

Week 12 - p-Values and Comparing Means

p-values

A p-value is, assuming H0 is true, the probability of a result at least as extreme as observed in the direction(s) of H1. Reject at level α when p≤α. It measures evidence against H0, not effect size.

Common misconceptions

p is not P(H0 true), and 1-p is not P(H1 true). Statistical significance is not practical importance.

Understanding check

Explain p=0.03 correctly.

One-sample t and CI connection

For unknown σ under normal sampling, T=(X̄-μ0)/(S/√n), df=n-1. For matching two-sided methods, H0:μ=μ0 is rejected at α exactly when μ0 falls outside the 100(1-α)% confidence interval.

Common misconceptions

Do not mix z intervals with t tests. The equivalence needs matching assumptions and a two-sided test.

Understanding check

How does a confidence interval identify null values not rejected?

Comparing two means

For independent samples, test μ1-μ2 using an appropriate SE. Under normal sampling with unknown equal variances, pooled variance is Sp²=[(n1-1)S1²+(n2-1)S2²]/(n1+n2-2). For paired data, test the within-pair mean difference.

Common misconceptions

Do not test each group separately and infer a difference. Do not use independent methods for matched observations.

Understanding check

Why is 'one significant and one not' not a test of their difference?
