Week 1 - Probability Foundations

Sample sₚaces and events

A sample sₚace \(S\) contains all possible outcomes. An event \(A\) is a subset of \(S\). Use \(A\cup B\) for “A or B”, \(A\cap B\) for “A and B”, and \(A^c\) for “not A”. De Morgan’s laws are:\[(A\cup B)^c=A^c\cap B^c,\qquad (A\cap B)^c=A^c\cup B^c.\]

Common misconceptions

Do not confuse an outcome with an event; do not assume OR is exclusive; define whether order matters.

Understanding check

Explain why the complement is useful for 'at least one' questions.

Counting and probability rules

For mutually exclusive cases, use the addition principle; across sequential stages, use the multiplication principle. Ordered selections of \(r\) objects from \(n\) use permutations, while unordered selections use combinations:\[P(n,r)=\frac{n!}{(n-r)!},\qquad {n\choose r}=\frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}.\]The main probability rules are:\[P(S)=1,\quad P(A)\ge 0,\quad P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)-P(A\cap B),\quad P(A^c)=1-P(A).\]

Common misconceptions

Do not add overlapping probabilities without subtracting the intersection. Do not use combinations when order matters.

Understanding check

When is \(P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)\) valid?

Week 2 - Conditional Probability and Bayes

Conditional probability

For events \(A\) and \(B\) with \(P(A)>0\), the conditional probability of \(B\) given \(A\) is:\[P(B\mid A)=\frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(A)}.\]Conditioning restricts the sample sₚace to outcomes where \(A\) occurred. The multiplication rule is:\[P(A\cap B)=P(A)P(B\mid A)=P(B)P(A\mid B).\]

Common misconceptions

\(P(A\mid B)\) and \(P(B\mid A)\) are different. Never assume conditioning leaves probability unchanged unless independence is established.

Understanding check

Give an example where \(P(A\mid B)\) is very different from \(P(B\mid A)\).

Independence

Events \(A\) and \(B\) are independent when:\[P(A\cap B)=P(A)P(B).\]When the relevant probabilities are positive, this is equivalent to \(P(A\mid B)=P(A)\) and \(P(B\mid A)=P(B)\). Mutually exclusive events with positive probability are not independent.

Common misconceptions

Mutually exclusive is not the same as independent. Pairwise independence need not imply mutual independence.

Understanding check

Can two positive-probability mutually exclusive events be independent?

Total probability and Bayes

If \(B_1,\ldots,B_k\) form a partition of the sample sₚace, then the law of total probability is:\[P(A)=\sum_{i=1}^{k}P(B_i)P(A\mid B_i).\]Bayes’ theorem updates a prior probability using observed evidence:\[P(B_j\mid A)=\frac{P(B_j)P(A\mid B_j)}{\sum_{i=1}^{k}P(B_i)P(A\mid B_i)}.\]

Common misconceptions

Do not ignore base rates in testing problems. Test sensitivity is not the same as probability of disease given a positive result.

Understanding check

Why can a highly accurate test produce many false positives for a rare condition?

Week 3 - Random Variables

Random variables and distributions

A random variable \(X\) maps outcomes to real numbers. A discrete random variable has a finite or countable range; a continuous random variable varies over intervals. For discrete \(X\), the probability mass function satisfies:\[p_X(x)=P(X=x),\qquad \sum_x p_X(x)=1.\]For continuous \(X\), probabilities are areas under a density \(f_X\), and \(P(X=x)=0\) for each individual point.

Common misconceptions

Do not treat a continuous density value as a point probability. Distinguish X, the random variable, from x, a realised value.

Understanding check

Why may a PDF exceed 1 even though probabilities cannot?

CDF, expectation, and variance

The cumulative distribution function is \(F_X(x)=P(X\le x)\). For continuous \(X\):\[F_X(x)=\int_{-\infty}^{x}f_X(t)\,dt.\]Expectation and variance are:\[E[X]=\sum_x x p_X(x)\quad\text{or}\quad E[X]=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}x f_X(x)\,dx,\]\[\operatorname{Var}(X)=E[X^2]-\bigl(E[X]\bigr)^2.\]For constants \(a,b\): \(E[aX+b]=aE[X]+b\) and \(\operatorname{Var}(aX+b)=a^2\operatorname{Var}(X)\).

Common misconceptions

E[g(X)] is generally not g(E[X]). Variance scales by a², not a. Standard deviation is the square root of variance.

Understanding check

Why does adding a constant change the mean but not the variance?

Week 4 - Joint Distributions and Dependence

Joint, marginal, and conditional distributions

A random vector \((X,Y)\) records two numerical features. From a joint density \(f_{X,Y}(x,y)\), obtain marginals by integrating out the other variable:\[f_X(x)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}f_{X,Y}(x,y)\,dy,\qquad f_Y(y)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}f_{X,Y}(x,y)\,dx.\]Where \(f_X(x)>0\), the conditional density is:\[f_{Y\mid X}(y\mid x)=\frac{f_{X,Y}(x,y)}{f_X(x)}.\]For discrete variables, replace integrals by sums.

Common misconceptions

Marginals do not uniquely determine the joint distribution. Always resₚect the support region.

Understanding check

How do you obtain \(f_X(x)\) from \(f_{X,Y}(x,y)\)?

Independence, covariance, correlation

Random variables \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent when their joint distribution factors into the product of their marginals. Covariance and correlation are:\[\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=E[XY]-E[X]E[Y],\]\[\rho_{X,Y}=\frac{\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)}{\sigma_X\sigma_Y},\qquad -1\le \rho_{X,Y}\le 1.\]Also:\[\operatorname{Var}(X+Y)=\operatorname{Var}(X)+\operatorname{Var}(Y)+2\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y).\]Independence implies zero covariance when the moments exist, but zero covariance does not generally imply independence.

Common misconceptions

Zero correlation does not prove independence. Correlation measures linear association, not causation or every nonlinear pattern.

Understanding check

Construct a dependent pair with zero covariance.

Week 5 - Discrete Models

Binomial

A binomial random variable \(X\sim\operatorname{Bin}(n,p)\) counts successes in \(n\) independent Bernoulli trials with constant success probability \(p\):\[P(X=x)={n\choose x}p^x(1-p)^{n-x},\qquad x=0,1,\ldots,n.\]\[E[X]=np,\qquad \operatorname{Var}(X)=np(1-p).\]

Common misconceptions

Check fixed n, two outcomes, constant p, and independence. Sampling without replacement from a small population is usually not binomial.

Understanding check

List the four binomial assumptions.

Geometric and negative binomial

A geometric random variable counts trials until the first success; a negative binomial random variable counts trials until the \(k\)-th success. Under the trial-count convention:\[E[X_{\mathrm{Geom}}]=\frac{1}{p},\qquad E[X_{\mathrm{NB}}]=\frac{k}{p}.\]The geometric distribution is memoryless:\[P(X>s+t\mid X>s)=P(X>t).\]

Common misconceptions

Be clear whether the convention counts failures or trials. Not every waiting-time distribution is memoryless.

Understanding check

Why does memorylessness matter in repeated-trial problems?

Poisson

A Poisson random variable \(X\sim\operatorname{Poi}(\lambda)\) counts independent events in a fixed interval at constant average rate \(\lambda\):\[P(X=x)=\frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^x}{x!},\qquad x=0,1,2,\ldots\]\[E[X]=\operatorname{Var}(X)=\lambda.\]Rates scale with interval length. Independent Poisson counts add, and independent thinning with retention probability \(p\) changes the rate from \(\lambda\) to \(p\lambda\).

Common misconceptions

Do not forget to rescale \(\lambda\) for a different interval. A matching mean and variance alone does not prove a Poisson process.

Understanding check

When is a Poisson approximation to Bin(n,p) reasonable?

Week 6 - Continuous Models

Uniform

X~U(a,b) has density 1/(b-a) on [a,b], mean (a+b)/2, and variance (b-a)²/12. Probability is interval length divided by total length.

Common misconceptions

Each individual point still has probability zero. Do not forget the total interval length.

Understanding check

Why is \(P(X=x)=0\) although \(X\) must take some value?

Exponential

For \(X\sim\operatorname{Exp}(\lambda)\):\[f_X(x)=\lambda e^{-\lambda x},\quad x\ge0;\qquad F_X(x)=1-e^{-\lambda x}.\]\[E[X]=\frac{1}{\lambda},\qquad \operatorname{Var}(X)=\frac{1}{\lambda^2}.\]It is memoryless:\[P(X>s+t\mid X>s)=P(X>t).\]

Common misconceptions

\(\lambda\) is a rate, not the mean. Memorylessness is a strong modelling assumption.

Understanding check

Convert a mean waiting time into the rate \(\lambda\).

Normal and approximation

A normal random variable is written \(X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)\). Standardise it using:\[Z=\frac{X-\mu}{\sigma}\sim N(0,1).\]A binomial distribution may be approximated by a normal distribution when both \(np\) and \(n(1-p)\) are sufficiently large. Apply a continuity correction because the binomial variable is discrete.

Common misconceptions

Do not confuse the standard deviation \(\sigma\) with the variance \(\sigma^2\). Apply continuity correction when approximating discrete probabilities.

Understanding check

Explain the continuity correction for \(P(X\le k)\).

Week 8 - Sampling, LLN, and CLT

Population, sample, statistic

A population is the full collection of interest; a sample is a subset. A simple random sample is selected by a random mechanism. A statistic is a function of sample data and is random before observing the sample; a parameter describes the population.

Common misconceptions

A parameter is not a statistic. Convenience samples are not automatically representative.

Understanding check

Distinguish the population mean \(\mu\) from the sample mean \(\bar X\).

Sampling distribution and standard error

For independent and identically distributed observations with mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2\), the sample mean \(\bar X\) satisfies:\[E[\bar X]=\mu,\qquad \operatorname{Var}(\bar X)=\frac{\sigma^2}{n},\qquad \operatorname{SE}(\bar X)=\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt n}.\]Standard error is the repeated-sampling sₚread of an estimator, not the sₚread of individual observations.

Common misconceptions

Do not confuse standard deviation with standard error. A larger sample does not change population variability.

Understanding check

What happens to \(\operatorname{SE}(\bar X)\) when \(n\) is multiplied by 4?

LLN and CLT

The Law of Large Numbers says \(\bar X\) approaches \(\mu\) as \(n\) grows. The Central Limit Theorem says that, under suitable conditions:\[\frac{\bar X-\mu}{\sigma/\sqrt n}\xrightarrow{d}N(0,1).\]For sufficiently large \(n\), the standardised sample mean is approximately standard normal. The theorem concerns the sampling distribution of \(\bar X\), not the distribution of individual observations.

Common misconceptions

The raw data do not become normal. CLT is not exact for every small sample or dependent dataset.

Understanding check

Contrast the LLN and CLT.

Week 9 - Sampling Distributions and Estimation

Chi-square

For a random sample from a normal population:\[\frac{(n-1)S^2}{\sigma^2}\sim\chi^2_{n-1}.\]The chi-square distribution is nonnegative and right-skewed. The exact result relies on the population being normal.

Common misconceptions

Use \(n-1\) degrees of freedom and keep \(S^2\) and \(\sigma^2\) squared. The exact result assumes normality.

Understanding check

What parameter can be inferred using this result?

Student t

For a normal population with unknown \(\sigma\):\[T=\frac{\bar X-\mu}{S/\sqrt n}\sim t_{n-1}.\]The Student \(t\) distribution is symmetric with heavier tails than the standard normal because \(\sigma\) is estimated. It approaches \(N(0,1)\) as the degrees of freedom increase.

Common misconceptions

The reason for the \(t\) distribution is that \(\sigma\) is estimated, not merely that the sample is small. Use \(n-1\) degrees of freedom.

Understanding check

Why are t tails heavier?

F distribution

If \(U_1\sim\chi^2_{\nu_1}\) and \(U_2\sim\chi^2_{\nu_2}\) are independent, then:\[F=\frac{U_1/\nu_1}{U_2/\nu_2}\sim F_{\nu_1,\nu_2}.\]Ratios of sample variances from independent normal populations lead to \(F\) procedures. The order of numerator and denominator degrees of freedom matters.

Common misconceptions

Numerator and denominator degrees of freedom are ordered and cannot be swapped casually.

Understanding check

What does an unusually large variance ratio suggest?

Point estimation and sample size

An estimator \(\hat\theta\) is unbiased for \(\theta\) when \(E[\hat\theta]=\theta\). For estimating a population mean with known \(\sigma\), the sample size required for margin of error \(E\) at confidence level \(1-\alpha\) is:\[n\ge\left(\frac{z_{\alpha/2}\sigma}{E}\right)^2.\]Always round the result up. Greater confidence or a smaller margin of error requires a larger sample.

Common misconceptions

Unbiased does not automatically mean low variance or best. Never round required n down.

Understanding check

Why must sample size be rounded up?

Week 10 - Confidence Intervals

One population mean

For a population mean with known \(\sigma\), a \(100(1-\alpha)\%\) confidence interval is:\[\bar X\pm z_{\alpha/2}\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt n}.\]For a normal population with unknown \(\sigma\):\[\bar X\pm t_{\alpha/2,n-1}\frac{S}{\sqrt n}.\]Confidence refers to the long-run success rate of the interval-producing procedure, not a probability assigned to the fixed parameter after the interval is calculated.

Common misconceptions

Do not say there is a 95% probability that \(\mu\) lies in an already observed interval. Choose \(z\) or \(t\) from the assumptions.

Understanding check

Give a correct interpretation of 95% confidence.

Two independent means

Estimate \(\mu_1-\mu_2\) using \(\bar X-\bar Y\). With known population variances, the standard error is:\[\operatorname{SE}(\bar X-\bar Y)=\sqrt{\frac{\sigma_1^2}{n_1}+\frac{\sigma_2^2}{n_2}}.\]For large samples with unknown variances, replace population variances by sample variances. Interpret the sign according to the chosen order of subtraction.

Common misconceptions

Do not reverse the subtraction order accidentally. Do not pool without an equal-variance assumption.

Understanding check

What does an interval entirely above zero imply?

Paired data

For paired observations, define the within-pair differences:\[D_i=X_i-Y_i.\]Then perform one-sample inference on the population mean difference \(\mu_D\), using \(\bar D\) and \(S_D/\sqrt n\). Pairing can remove variation caused by differences between matched units.

Common misconceptions

Before/after data are not independent samples. Do not ignore natural pairs.

Understanding check

Why can pairing improve precision?

Week 11 - Hypothesis Testing

Hypotheses and test direction

A hypothesis test compares a null hypothesis \(H_0\) with an alternative \(H_1\). The null normally contains equality and concerns a population parameter. For example, testing whether a population mean exceeds 50 uses:\[H_0:\mu=50\quad\text{versus}\quad H_1:\mu>50.\]The direction must be chosen from the research question before insₚecting the data.

Common misconceptions

Do not choose the direction after seeing the data. Hypotheses should be written about population parameters such as \(\mu\), not the sample statistic \(\bar X\).

Understanding check

Write hypotheses for testing whether \(\mu>50\).

Type I, Type II, and power

A Type I error rejects a true \(H_0\), with probability \(\alpha\). A Type II error fails to reject a false \(H_0\), with probability \(\beta\) at a sₚecified alternative. Power is:\[\operatorname{Power}=1-\beta.\]Larger samples generally increase power, all else equal.

Common misconceptions

Failure to reject is not proof of H0. Power depends on effect size and sample size.

Understanding check

Explain the trade-off between \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\).

Test statistics and decisions

For testing a mean when \(\sigma\) is known, the test statistic is:\[Z=\frac{\bar X-\mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt n}.\]Reject \(H_0\) when the statistic lies in the rejection region determined by \(\alpha\) and \(H_1\). State the conclusion as evidence about the population claim, not as certainty.

Common misconceptions

Do not compare raw means with z critical values. Say 'fail to reject', not 'accept', H0.

Understanding check

What determines whether one or two tails are used?

Week 12 - p-Values and Comparing Means

p-values

A p-value is the probability, assuming \(H_0\) is true, of observing a test statistic at least as extreme as the one obtained, in the direction or directions sₚecified by \(H_1\). The decision rule is:\[\text{Reject }H_0\text{ at level }\alpha\quad\text{if}\quad p\le\alpha.\]A p-value measures evidence against \(H_0\); it is neither the probability that \(H_0\) is true nor an effect size.

Common misconceptions

\(p\) is not \(P(H_0\text{ is true})\), and \(1-p\) is not \(P(H_1\text{ is true})\). Statistical significance is not practical importance.

Understanding check

Explain p=0.03 correctly.

One-sample t and CI connection

For a normal population with unknown \(\sigma\), test \(H_0:\mu=\mu_0\) using:\[T=\frac{\bar X-\mu_0}{S/\sqrt n},\qquad T\sim t_{n-1}\text{ under }H_0.\]For matching two-sided methods, \(H_0:\mu=\mu_0\) is rejected at level \(\alpha\) exactly when \(\mu_0\) lies outside the corresₚonding \(100(1-\alpha)\%\) confidence interval.

Common misconceptions

Do not mix z intervals with t tests. The equivalence needs matching assumptions and a two-sided test.

Understanding check

How does a confidence interval identify null values not rejected?

Comparing two means

For independent samples, test a claim about \(\mu_1-\mu_2\) using the appropriate standard error. Under independent normal sampling with unknown but equal variances, the pooled variance is:\[S_p^2=\frac{(n_1-1)S_1^2+(n_2-1)S_2^2}{n_1+n_2-2}.\]The pooled two-sample test statistic for \(H_0:\mu_1-\mu_2=\Delta_0\) is:\[T=\frac{(\bar X-\bar Y)-\Delta_0}{S_p\sqrt{1/n_1+1/n_2}},\qquad \mathrm{df}=n_1+n_2-2.\]For paired data, instead test the mean of the within-pair differences.

Common misconceptions

Do not test each group separately and infer a difference. Do not use independent methods for matched observations.

Understanding check

Why is 'one significant and one not' not a test of their difference?

Expanded Reference and Method Selection Guide

These additions consolidate recurring rules and distinctions from the full ST2334 notes into a format that SureBo can retrieve quickly when assessing a learner.

Parameter, statistic, estimator, and estimate

A parameter is a fixed but usually unknown numerical feature of a population, such as \(\mu\), \(\sigma^2\), or \(p\). A statistic is a function of the observed sample and contains no unknown population parameter. An estimator is a statistic used before observing data to estimate a parameter. An estimate is the realised numerical value after data are observed.

Common trap

Do not call \(\bar X\) a population parameter, and do not call the unknown \(\mu\) a statistic.

Bias, variance, and mean squared error

\[ \operatorname{Bias}(\hat\theta)=E[\hat\theta]-\theta \]
\[ \operatorname{MSE}(\hat\theta)=\operatorname{Var}(\hat\theta)+\operatorname{Bias}(\hat\theta)^2 \]

An unbiased estimator has zero bias, but it is not automatically the best estimator. Precision also depends on variance. MSE combines both considerations.

Standard deviation versus standard error

Standard deviation describes variation among individual observations. Standard error describes repeated-sampling variation of a statistic.

\[ \operatorname{SE}(\bar X)=\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt n} \quad\text{or estimated by}\quad \frac{S}{\sqrt n} \]

Multiplying \(n\) by four halves the standard error. To divide standard error by three, multiply \(n\) by nine.

Choosing between \(z\) and \(t\)

The \(t\) distribution has heavier tails because replacing \(\sigma\) by \(S\) introduces additional uncertainty.

Confidence interval mechanics

\[ \text{estimate}\ \pm\ \text{critical value}\times\text{standard error} \]

The midpoint is the point estimate. Increasing confidence increases the critical value and widens the interval. Increasing sample size reduces standard error and narrows the interval. Changing confidence level does not change the midpoint or the observed standard error.

Hypothesis-test decision language

Use reject \(H_0\) or fail to reject \(H_0\). Do not say that the data “prove” either hypothesis. A failure to reject means the evidence was insufficient at the chosen significance level.

\[ p\le \alpha \Rightarrow \text{reject }H_0, \qquad p>\alpha \Rightarrow \text{fail to reject }H_0. \]

Correct p-value interpretation

The p-value is calculated under the assumption that \(H_0\) is true. It is the probability of a test statistic at least as extreme as the observed statistic in the direction sₚecified by \(H_1\).

It is not \(P(H_0\text{ is true})\), not \(P(H_1\text{ is true})\), and not a measure of practical importance.

Transformations and linear combinations

\[ E[aX+bY+c]=aE[X]+bE[Y]+c \]
\[ \operatorname{Var}(aX+bY) =a^2\operatorname{Var}(X)+b^2\operatorname{Var}(Y) +2ab\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y) \]

If \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent, the covariance term is zero. Zero covariance alone does not generally establish independence.

Quantiles and upper-tail notation

Always check the convention used in the notes. If \(z_\alpha\) is an upper-tail quantile, then

\[ P(Z>z_\alpha)=\alpha. \]

Likewise, \(t_{\nu;\alpha}\) and \(F_{\nu_1,\nu_2;\alpha}\) commonly denote upper-tail critical values. Mixing upper-tail and lower-tail conventions changes signs and probabilities.

Assumption audit before inference

  • Were observations selected or generated independently?
  • Is the sample representative of the target population?
  • Is normality required because the sample is small?
  • For pooling, is the equal-variance assumption justified?
  • Is the data structure independent or paired?
  • Is the direction of the alternative hypothesis defined before insₚecting data?
